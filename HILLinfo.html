How the Hill Cipher Works

First in the Hill Cipher, a square, invertible matrix must be determined (this ideally would be randomized). Let's say there is a matrix A which is \(nxn\) and the message wishing to be encrypted is then a matrix M which is \(nx1\). For this M matrix, the values need to correspond to the letters in the message, rather by the corresponding number in the alphabet, ASCII values, or some other method. The message can be split up into pieces n letters long.

To determine the encrypted message, use matrix multiplication to multiply \(A \cdot M\). Then, take that result, \(AM \pmod{v}\), where v is however many numerical values there are which correspond to a letter (so 26, assuming only letters are being encrypted).

To decrypt the message, use the inverse of the matix A in mod 26, and multiply \(A^{-1} \cdot E\) where E is the encrypted message. Then, take \(A^{-1}E\pmod{v}\) where v is the same as in the encryption step.

For the sake of this example, a hill cipher that employs a 3x3 matrix will be discussed. The matrix can be any size, but it must always be square. This is an example using a randomized matrix from our code: \begin{bmatrix} 23& 4& 15 \\ 7& 0& 10 \\ 17& 25& 11 \\ \end{bmatrix} In this basic scheme, each letter in the key is associated with a number in the matrix based off the letter of that alphabet at that position (A is associated with 0, B with 1, C with 2, etc). In reality, the number associated with each number can be randomized. This matrix represents the key: XEPHAKRZL. The message we are trying to encode will be split into groups of 3 characters, since there are 3 columns in the matrix. Let "XYZ" be the first three characters of the theoretical message. This coincides to the numbers 23, 24, and 25. Use these to form a vector. This answer is then converted to modulo 26. This is necessary to be able to associate each resulting number to a number in the alphabet, since there are 26 letters.

\( \begin{bmatrix} 23& 4& 15 \\ 7& 0& 10 \\ 17& 25& 11 \\ \end{bmatrix} * \begin{bmatrix} 23\\ 24\\ 25\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1122\\ 717\\ 860\\ \end{bmatrix} \pmod{26} = \begin{bmatrix} 4\\ 15\\ 2\\ \end{bmatrix} \)

If we look at the letter associated with each of these numbers, the encrypted message is: EPC. To decrypt, multiply the vector representation of the encrypted message by the inverse of the key in modulo 26. The calculated matrix will correspond to the original message, "XYZ."

\( \begin{bmatrix} 23& 7& 17 \\ 4& 0& 25 \\ 15& 10& 11 \\ \end{bmatrix} ^{-1} \pmod{26} \begin{bmatrix} 4\\ 15\\ 2\\ \end{bmatrix} \)

\( = \begin{bmatrix} 12& 5& 15 \\ 15& 8& 13 \\ 22& 19& 8 \\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4\\ 15\\ 12\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 153\\ 206\\ 389\\ \end{bmatrix} \pmod{26} = \begin{bmatrix} 23\\ 24\\ 25\\ \end{bmatrix} \)
