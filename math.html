Modular Arithmetic

Modular arithmetic in this situation is essentially looking for the remainder when dividing by the modulus (the value n in (mod n)).

For example, when looking at modulo 3, the remainder could be 0, 1, or 2. \(6 \pmod 3 = 0\), \(7 \pmod 3 = 1\), \(8 \pmod 3 = 2\). This can be done for larger numbers, most easily found using long division. To find \(31 \pmod 5\), first divide \(31 \div 5 = 6.2\). Then, take the whole number value, and multiply it by the modulo: \(5 \cdot 6 = 30\), then \(31-30=1\), which then gives the answer for \(31 \pmod 5 = 1\).

For more information about this, visit Modular Numbers and Cryptography for a detailed explaination by Matthew Cheung (Oct 21, 2021).

Matrix Multiplication

For matrix multiplication, the product must be computed using the row-column rule. Lets look at \(A \times B\), where:

\(A = \begin{bmatrix} a_1& a_2 \\ a_3& a_4 \\ \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} b_1& b_2 \\ b_3& b_4 \\ \end{bmatrix}\).

Then \(A \times B\) \(= \begin{bmatrix} a_1b_1 + a_2b_3 & a_1b_2 + a_2b_4 \\ a_3b_1 + a_4b_3 & a_3b_2 + a_4b_4\\ \end{bmatrix}\)

In order for two matrices to be mutliplied, the dimentions \(n \times m\) and \(m \times q\), m must be the same. The inside dimentions must match, so matrices in the order \((m \times q)\times(n \times m)\) can only be multiplied if \(q = n\). See the image below for more.
